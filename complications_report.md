# PEARS Surgery Complication & Side Effect Analysis

While PEARS is widely reported to be less traumatic than a traditional root replacement, it is still major open-chest surgery. Based on a scan of all 54 patient stories, here is a summary of the most frequently reported post-operative complications and side effects.

## 1. Most Common Post-Op Issues

### Arrhythmia & Atrial Fibrillation (AFib)
This is the most frequently cited significant complication. Many patients experience a rapid or irregular heartbeat, often occurring between Day 3 and Week 2 post-op. 
*   **Resolution:** In almost all cases, this is quickly resolved using medications like beta-blockers or Amiodarone. In rare, severe cases (like Patient Pavel), electric cardioversion was required.

### Temperature Spikes / Fevers
Many patients (e.g., Joshua, Martyn, Jodie) report spiking a fever within the first few days. 
*   **Resolution:** Nurses often clarify this is an inflammatory response to the surgery and the new implant, rather than an infection. It usually passes within 48 hours but can delay hospital discharge by a day.

### Nausea & Vomiting
A direct reaction to general anesthesia and strong painkillers (morphine/fentanyl). 
*   **Resolution:** Most severe on Day 1. Anti-sickness medication is provided, and the nausea generally subsides by Day 2 or 3 as you switch to oral painkillers.

## 2. Fluid Issues

### Pericardial & Pleural Effusion
Fluid buildup around the heart (pericardial) or lungs (pleural) is a common side effect of chest surgery. 
*   **Resolution:** Often picked up on routine X-rays or CT scans and treated with diuretics or anti-inflammatories (like Ibuprofen for pericarditis).

### General Fluid Retention
Some patients note gaining significant weight (e.g., 3+ kg) immediately after surgery due to fluid retention, which is flushed out over the next week. 

## 3. Pain & Discomfort Anomalies

### Chest Drain Removal
While general chest pain is reported to be surprisingly low ("more like discomfort"), almost every patient notes that the removal of the chest drainage tubes (usually Day 2 or 3) is a distinctly sharp, painful experience. 
*   **Tip:** Press your pain relief button (PCA) 15-30 minutes *before* the nurse removes the drains.

### Back, Neck, and Shoulder Pain
Because the sternum is healing, you are required to sleep flat on your back for up to 6 weeks. This forced sleeping posture causes significant back and neck pain for many patients. 
*   **Tip:** Having a V-shaped pillow or a wedge to sleep at a 45-degree angle helps immensely.

## 4. Psychological Impact

### Anxiety, Depression, and Nightmares
The mental toll of heart surgery is frequently mentioned. 
*   Some patients report sudden bouts of depression or anxiety in the first week. 
*   Medications can cause vivid, lucid nightmares or hallucinations (especially in the ICU). 
*   Patient Pavel experienced severe PTSD post-surgery, requiring psychiatric support, highlighting the importance of mental healthcare during recovery.

> [!WARNING]
> If you experience a sudden racing heart (AFib) after you have been discharged home, **do not panic**, but you should seek immediate medical attention (A&E or ER) to have it checked and medicated. Do not wait for a GP appointment.
