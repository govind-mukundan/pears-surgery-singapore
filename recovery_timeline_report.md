# PEARS Surgery Recovery Timeline Summary

Based on the analysis of 54 patient stories, here is a consolidated timeline of what you can expect during your recovery from PEARS surgery.

## In-Hospital Recovery (Average Stay: 4 to 8 Days)

*   **Day 0 (Surgery):** The surgery typically takes 2-3 hours. You will wake up in the Intensive Care Unit (ICU). Most patients report feeling disoriented and having various tubes (breathing, chest drains, catheter) but cite that the pain is often well-managed by a morphine button (PCA).
*   **Day 1 to 2:** Transfer to the High Dependency Unit (HDU) or a regular ward. You will be encouraged to stand up and walk short distances (e.g., to the bathroom) with assistance.
*   **Day 3 to 4:** Removal of most tubes and drains. Many patients describe this as a major "turning point" where they feel significantly better. You will start taking short walks around the hospital ward.
*   **Day 5 to 7:** Discharge from the hospital. The most common discharge day reported across all stories is **Day 6**.

## Early At-Home Recovery (Weeks 1 to 3)

*   **Week 1:** You will likely need significant rest and regular naps. Sleep can be difficult because you are required to sleep on your back (often elevated at a 45-degree angle) to protect your healing sternum.
*   **Week 2:** Daily walks become a vital part of recovery. Patients report walking increasing distances each day. Pain medication is usually tapered down to just paracetamol or stopped entirely.
*   **Week 3:** Noticeable return of energy and strength. Many patients are walking several miles a day by this point. 

## Mid-to-Late Recovery (Weeks 4 to 12)

*   **Weeks 4 to 6:** You will typically be cleared to drive again at the 6-week mark. You should still avoid lifting anything heavy (more than half a kettle of water) to allow the sternum to fully heal. Cardiac rehabilitation programs often begin around this time.
*   **Months 2 to 3:** Full return to normal life. Most patients return to full-time work and are cleared for strenuous activities, including swimming, going to the gym, and even contact sports.

> [!TIP]
> **Key Takeaway for Living Alone:** The first two weeks at home are the most challenging. You will be restricted from lifting heavy items and may need assistance getting in and out of bed or the bath due to the sternum restrictions. It is highly recommended to have someone stay with you or assist you daily for at least the first 10-14 days after discharge.
