PEARS Patient Story: Jodie Stringer
Source: https://exstent.com/story/jodie-stringer/
Condition: Marfan Syndrome, aorta went from 41mm to 48mm in 3-4 months post-pregnancy, prior mitral valve prolapse concerns
Location: Sheffield, England; surgery at Guy's & St Thomas' (London)
Surgeon: Mr. Conal Austin
Age: 34
Profession: GP (General Practitioner)

================================================================================

BACKGROUND:
Has Marfan syndrome, also a GP in England. Told she had 'mild' form. Underwent IVF with pre-genetic disease testing, delivered healthy Marfan-free baby July 2019. Heart scans fine throughout pregnancy but routine monitoring scan found aorta went from 41mm to 48mm within 3-4 months. Contacted Mr. Austin's secretary urgently. He said she was perfect candidate. Frustrated that cardiologist had refused referral years earlier. Delayed by COVID, got date in July.

DAY 1 (SURGERY DAY):
- Surgery in afternoon, nursing staff got her ready in morning in case of early slot
- Told going down at 11 but didn't until 2pm (frustrating)
- Jovial atmosphere in anesthesia room, given sedative
- Multiple lines put in while asleep (don't remember - great!)
- Husband contacted ~5pm by Mr. Austin to say she was okay
- First memory: vaguely seeing several heads, then 7pm
- Doesn't remember ventilator removal (done in theatre)
- BAD REACTION TO ANESTHESIA: horrendous nausea and vomiting, several anti-sickness meds with limited effect
- Pain button was great but reluctant to press due to sickness
- Her level of sickness described as "very rare"

DAY 2:
- Still felt very sick but no longer vomiting
- Moved from ITU to HDU afternoon
- Day varied between drug-induced sleep and awake feeling sick
- Kept sending jumbled texts to husband

DAY 3:
- Finally on top of nausea, managed to eat, felt much better
- Still drug-induced sleep but better in herself
- ALL LINES CAME OUT: chest drain uncomfortable but only for a second - "felt like a new woman"
- Arterial line (wrist) and central line (neck) didn't hurt at all
- Moving immediately much easier, more confident sitting up
- Changed pain button to oral medications only (joint choice) - cleared her head
- Walked independently to ward - felt great

DAY 4:
- No sickness, stopped sickness tablets
- Struggled with pain more (because moving more)
- IMPORTANT: Ask nursing team for pain meds, need pain controlled enough to cough and take deep breaths
- Rolled up towel against breastbone = "your best friend"
- Spiked temperature 38.6°C for ~12 hours - inflammatory reaction to PEARS, NOT infection
- Evening: suddenly didn't feel unwell anymore - "the turning point"

DAY 5:
- Chest X-ray and echo, kept in to monitor temperature (stayed down)
- Transport needed 24 hours to organise (London to Sheffield via hospital transport due to COVID shielding)

DAY 6:
- HOME! Journey wasn't too bad. Got home and went to bed.

DAY 7:
- Felt really poorly - travelling had exhausted her, body needed rest. Hard day. Quickly improved.

WEEK 2:
- Kept things steady, felt much better at home
- Quickly weaned off painkillers
- Still resting and sleeping a lot

WEEK 3:
- Felt much better, started 20-min flat slow walks around garden
- Only sternum discomfort slowing her down

WEEKS 4-6:
- More walking, more feeling normal, energy returning
- Came better than expected
- Biggest issue: doing too much or knocking sternum (happened a lot with managing 1-year-old!)
- Had guests and friends around

17 WEEKS POST-OP:
- Back to work as normal
- Back to outdoor activities
- Could carry 1-year-old on back, walking in Peak District

1 YEAR POST-OP:
- No problems, back to normal
- Occasional niggles from sternal wires
- Scar much fainter

3 YEARS POST-OP (NOW):
- No complications or issues
- All follow-up scans fine
- Aorta now measures ~45mm on annual MRI
- Runs, hikes, climbs, carries preschooler without issues
- Very rarely some sternal irritation
- Only reminder is faded scar

COMPLICATIONS:
- Severe nausea/vomiting from anesthesia (rare severity)
- Temperature spike 38.6°C (inflammatory reaction, not infection)

PRACTICAL ADVICE:
1. Bring comfortable PJs easy to get on/off
2. Audiobooks great for first few days
3. Ear plugs and eye mask for sleeping on wards
4. Sitting up and moving as soon as possible helps recovery
5. Accept rolled towel to hold against chest when walking/using toilet
6. Don't get constipated!

ADVOCATE FOR YOURSELF: Push your cardiologist for a referral to someone who does PEARS.
