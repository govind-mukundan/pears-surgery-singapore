CONSOLIDATED RECOVERY SUMMARY FOR SOMEONE LIVING ALONE
=======================================================

HOSPITAL STAY (expect 4-7 days)
-------------------------------
All patients stayed 4-7 days in hospital:
- Kevin Conway: 5 days
- Scott Stowell: 5 days (Christmas Eve discharge)
- Dennis Lieskamp: 6 days
- Esther Nobel-Renes: 6 days (1 day ICU)
- Beirne Chisolm: 4 days
- Michael Boecker: 8 days (Sunday discharge)
- Joe Madden: 6 days
- Marin Mircea: 7 days
- Christine Velasquez: ~4-5 days
- Nicole Boyce: Not specified but implied ~1 week

FIRST 24-48 HOURS (IN HOSPITAL)
---------------------------------
- ICU stay typically 1 day, sometimes overnight only
- Breathing tube removed usually same day or day 2
- Multiple tubes/lines: arms, chest, stomach, neck, urine catheter
- Night sweats common (multiple patients report this)
- Minimal to moderate pain (most describe "discomfort" not "pain")
- Nausea and loss of appetite common
- Shallow breathing / difficulty breathing
- Back pain from lying on back
- LOW BLOOD PRESSURE reported by some
- TACHYCARDIA episode reported by one patient (Scott)

DAYS 2-4 (IN HOSPITAL)
------------------------
- Standing and walking begins (usually Day 2-3)
- Physio therapy / exercises begin
- Tubes gradually removed
- FIRST SHOWER is a major milestone (Day 3-4) - described as life-changing
- Appetite slowly returns
- Coughing is necessary (and painful) to clear fluid from lungs
- Use a SPIROMETER consistently

IMMEDIATE POST-DISCHARGE (Weeks 1-2)
--------------------------------------
- MUST SLEEP ELEVATED ON BACK (pillows arranged, or armchair)
- Night sweats continue for some (clothing changes every 2 hours for one patient)
- Walking is the primary exercise: start with short walks, extend gradually
- Most patients walking 1+ miles within first week post-discharge
- Christine walked 6 miles at Day 7
- Beirne walking along Thames daily
- Kevin walking 1km on treadmill day of discharge
- Fatigue / need for naps
- CORSET/SUPPORT required (still worn at 3 months for one patient)
- Keep physical activity "LIGHT" for at least 6 weeks
- "Light" = can "walk and talk" at same time

WEEKS 2-6 POST-SURGERY
------------------------
- Gradual strength improvement
- Some patients back to light work within 2-3 weeks
- Kevin: working in office 80% at 2.5 weeks
- Beirne: jumping rope at 3 weeks
- Continue daily walking, gradually increasing distance
- Sleeping position can gradually become less elevated

COMPLICATIONS TO BE AWARE OF (CRITICAL FOR SOLO PATIENTS)
-----------------------------------------------------------
1. PERICARDIAL EFFUSION (fluid around heart)
   - Dennis: readmitted at 3 WEEKS with atrial fibrillation from this
   - Michael: found during rehab checkup
   - Required drain insertion and temporary medication
   *** THIS IS THE #1 RISK FOR SOMEONE LIVING ALONE - can happen weeks later ***

2. PLEURAL EFFUSION (fluid around lungs)
   - Michael: found during rehab
   - Resolved with treatment

3. ATRIAL FIBRILLATION / ARRHYTHMIA
   - Dennis: caused by pericardial effusion at 3 weeks
   - Marin: minor episodes, self-resolved
   - Michael: not specified but multiple medications needed

4. PARTIALLY COLLAPSED LUNG
   - Kevin: not uncommon especially with Marfan patients
   - Requires deep breathing and coughing exercises

5. FEVER
   - Michael: intermittent fever for days post-surgery
   - Marin: max 38°C

6. NIGHT SWEATS
   - Kevin, Marin: significant night sweats
   - Marin changed clothes every 2 hours at night

7. COVID/ILLNESS RISK
   - Scott: got sick BEFORE surgery (London Tube)
   - Michael: caught COVID during rehab, reset recovery

8. BACK PAIN
   - Michael: "very serious" back pain for 2 weeks from sleeping on back only

WHAT YOU NEED ARRANGED AS SOMEONE LIVING ALONE
================================================

BEFORE SURGERY:
- Someone to accompany you to hospital and be your contact person
- Every patient had family/spouse with them
- Accommodation near hospital with kitchen facilities for recovery
- Pre-arrange pillow structure at home for sleeping elevated
- Stock up: healthy fluids, easy foods, bone broth, coconut water
- Get: heating pad, massage gun, long phone charging cable, cough drops
- Get: spirometer for breathing exercises
- Arrange transport home from hospital (you won't be driving)

FIRST 2 WEEKS POST-DISCHARGE:
- STRONGLY RECOMMEND having someone stay with you or check on you daily
- You will need help with: showering (initially), meal preparation, laundry
- You cannot lift anything heavy
- You will tire easily and need naps
- Night sweats mean frequent clothing/bedding changes

WEEKS 2-6:
- CRITICAL: Be aware of delayed complications (especially pericardial effusion at ~3 weeks)
- Know warning signs: irregular heartbeat, chest pressure, shortness of breath, fever
- Have a plan to get to hospital quickly if needed
- Continue daily walks, gradually increasing
- May need help with shopping/errands
- Rehab program recommended if available

AFTER 6 WEEKS:
- Most patients largely independent
- Gradual return to normal activities
- Some patients back to full exercise by 8 weeks (Beirne)
- Others take 3-6 months for full recovery
- Some wear corset for months

OVERALL OUTLOOK:
- ALL patients in these stories had positive outcomes
- Most describe recovery as better than expected
- No lifelong medication needed (unlike valve replacement alternatives)
- Multiple patients running, swimming, cycling, gym within months
- Joe Madden: 3+ years out, fully active, no medication
